Listening to the East Bay Hills

LIVE MONITORING · East Bay Hills, CA  ·  Expanding to NorCal and East Coast

We currently have listening stations deployed across the East Bay hills, running overnight to capture the full acoustic picture of local wildlife. Frogs, owls, bats, insects — anything that makes a sound gets logged, classified, and mapped.

The system runs 24/7 without human oversight. When an animal calls, a small on-device AI identifies the species in under a second — without sending any audio to the cloud. The Monitor and Map pages above show what's being detected in near real-time, updated every 15 minutes.

We're planning to expand monitoring work to other parts of Northern California and eventually do East Coast testing. Each new site teaches the system what normal sounds like so unusual activity stands out.

How It Works

Real Brains, No Cloud

Most wildlife monitors send audio to a remote server for analysis — which means gaps, costs, and privacy concerns. CricketNode runs its own AI directly on a small computer at the edge of the field.

01

Listen

Microphones record continuously through the night. A hub node coordinates a mesh of smaller field nodes — each covering a different part of the site.

02

Identify

The moment something is detected, an AI model trained on thousands of species runs locally — on the device itself. No internet connection needed. No audio ever leaves the property.

03

Map & Report

Detections are pinned to a live map with GPS coordinates, confidence scores, and audio clips you can play back. A nightly report summarizes what was found and flags anything unusual.

04

Act

Pest pressure detected early means farmers can respond before damage occurs — with targeted, evidence-based intervention instead of blanket spraying.

Ecosystem · Coming Next

Detection Is Just the Start

CricketNode tells you what's there. The next piece of the system is what to do about it.

🔊

MantisGuard — Acoustic Deterrence

A companion unit that responds to detections in real time with targeted acoustic deterrents — species-specific sounds that discourage pests without chemicals, without harming beneficial species, and without bothering neighbors. CricketNode feeds MantisGuard the data it needs to act precisely.

About

Built by One Person

Steady Acres is a solo project by Jack Beautz — software engineer and hobbyist naturalist based in the Bay Area. I built CricketNode because I wanted to know what was happening on a piece of land I care about after dark, and couldn't find a tool that was private, affordable, and actually smart.

Everything you see here — the hardware, the AI pipeline, the dashboard — is running on custom-built hardware in the field right now. If you're a farmer, land manager, researcher, or just curious, feel free to reach out.
